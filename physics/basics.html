Basic Physics of Rowing

Understanding the fundamental physics principles that govern rowing mechanics, from propulsion and resistance to boat dynamics and energy efficiency.

1. Propulsion

Why does the blade have to slip in the water?

Consider a boat before and after a stroke. Before the stroke, total momentum p = 0, since everything is at rest. After the stroke, total momentum must still equal zero because the total momentum can't change (Newton's 2nd Law).

Conservation of Momentum:

Total momentum: p = mbvb - mwvw = 0

Where: mb = boat+crew mass, vb = boat velocity, mw = water mass, vw = water velocity

Example

For a boat+crew mass mb = 100 kg (single sculler) to accelerate from rest to vb = 1 m/s, requires either:

  • mw = 10 kg water accelerated to vw = 10 m/s, or
  • mw = 20 kg water accelerated to vw = 5 m/s

Any combination where mwvw = mbvb = 100 kg⋅m/s works.

During the normal stroke with the boat already moving, it's less obvious that water moves backwards to keep the boat moving forwards since the blades appear to 'lock' where they're placed. However, if you look at the puddles when blades are extracted, it's clear that water is moved.

There has to be some slippage to accelerate the boat, although from energy considerations this should be minimized.

Note: If you could push off the river bottom or poles along the bank instead of water, the whole planet would move backwards instead, and some slippage would still occur (detectable by a very pedantic spaceman with precise measuring equipment).

2. Resistance

What causes a boat to slow down?

Bodies moving in fluids slow down due to resistive forces known as drag. This represents a transfer of momentum from the body to the fluid: the surrounding fluid speeds up as the body slows down, maintaining constant total momentum.

Types of Drag for Boats

  • Skin Drag: Friction between hull and water (≈80% for racing shells)
  • Form Drag: Turbulence from hull passage
  • Wave Drag: Energy lost creating waves

Air Resistance

  • Few % in still air
  • Can rise to 10s of % in strong headwinds
  • Much more variable than water resistance

Why don't you go twice as fast if you pull twice as hard?

Skin drag is proportional to the square of velocity. Assuming skin drag dominates:

Resistance Formula:

R = a⋅v²

Where R = resistance, v = velocity, a = constant (depends on wetted surface area and hull shape)

To maintain constant velocity, applied force must equal resistance (Newton's 1st Law). Therefore, average power required is:

Power Formula:

P = a⋅v³

Power = Force × velocity = R × v = a⋅v² × v = a⋅v³

Key Insight

To double boat speed, you need 2³ = 8 times more power.

If you double power, you only go 2^(1/3) = 1.26 times as fast.

This is why rowing firm pressure doesn't get you past a crew rowing light pressure as quickly as you'd hope.

3. Kinetic Energy

Why are 'cleavers' a good idea?

From the propulsion example, both scenarios achieve the same boat speed, but they require different amounts of energy. The total kinetic energy of the system is:

U = ½mbvb² + ½mwvw²

Scenario Water Mass Water Velocity Total Energy
Small blade, fast water 10 kg 10 m/s 550 Joules
Large blade, slow water 20 kg 5 m/s 300 Joules

Energy Efficiency Principle

To achieve a given increase in boat speed, it takes less energy to move a large amount of water slowly than a small amount of water quickly.

This is the basic argument in favor of 'bigger is better' spoon sizes and for not washing out.

4. Centre of Mass

Why do boats continue to accelerate after the stroke ends?

If a crew (mass mc) sits still at backstops in a boat (mass mb) moving at velocity vt, the total momentum is (mc + mb)vt.

When the crew moves sternwards at velocity -vc relative to the boat, the boat must move bowwards at velocity vb to conserve momentum:

mcvt + mbvt = mc(vt - vc) + mb(vt + vb)

Simplifying: mcvc = mbvb

Practical Example

If the crew is 80% of total mass (mc = 4mb), then vb = 4vc.

If crew moves sternwards at vc = 0.2 m/s, the boat moves bowwards an extra 0.8 m/s.

In 1 second: to the crew, they've moved 1m toward stern; to an observer, 80cm was the boat moving toward the crew.

Why Boats Surge After Finish

Although blades have been extracted and no longer accelerate the system's center of mass, the crew's movement off backstops accelerates the hull forwards by equal and opposite reaction.

5. Speed Variation

Why is constant pace more efficient?

Consider rowing 1 minute at 4 m/s, then 1 minute at 6 m/s versus 2 minutes at constant 5 m/s. Both cover 600m total distance.

Variable Speed (4 m/s then 6 m/s)

Using P = av³ with a = 1 kg/m for simple calculation:

Total work = 60 × 4³ + 60 × 6³ = 60 × 64 + 60 × 216 = 16,800 Joules

Average power = 140 Watts

Constant Speed (5 m/s)

Total work = 60 × 5³ + 60 × 5³ = 2 × 60 × 125 = 15,000 Joules

Average power = 125 Watts

Efficiency Principle

Constant pace uses less average power (15% less in this example) to cover the same distance in the same time.

It's more energy-efficient to maintain the same pace throughout a race rather than starting fast and slowing down, or starting slow and speeding up.

Hull Speed Variation During Stroke

The same principle applies to hull speed variation during each stroke. Since skin drag resistance depends on hull speed rather than center-of-mass speed, minimizing hull speed variation throughout the stroke is more efficient.

Sliding Rigger Boats: These were banned because they reduced hull speed variation (sculler's seat fixed to hull, stretcher and riggers slide), making them theoretically more efficient. The theory presumably worked since they're now prohibited.
